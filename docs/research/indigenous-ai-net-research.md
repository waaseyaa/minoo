## 1. What Is the Indigenous Protocol and AI Working Group?

The Indigenous Protocol and Artificial Intelligence (IP AI) Working Group develops new conceptual and practical approaches to building the next generation of AI systems. It was initiated in 2019 with two workshops in Hawai'i, bringing together 35 individuals from Indigenous communities across Aotearoa, Australia, North America, and the Pacific.

The group's central questions:
- From an Indigenous perspective, what should our relationship with AI be?
- How can Indigenous epistemologies and ontologies contribute to the global conversation regarding society and AI?
- How do we broaden discussions regarding the role of technology in society beyond the largely culturally homogeneous research labs and Silicon Valley startup culture?
- How do we imagine a future with AI that contributes to the flourishing of all humans and non-humans?

## 4. The Position Paper

**Title:** *Indigenous Protocol and Artificial Intelligence Position Paper*
**Published:** 2020 (spring)
**Length:** 205 pages
**Download:** Available from Concordia University's Spectrum repository in [English](https://spectrum.library.concordia.ca/id/eprint/986506/) and [ʻŌlelo Hawaiʻi](https://spectrum.library.concordia.ca/id/eprint/990094/)
**Also published as a book:** ISBN 9781387659258 (available on Amazon)

### What It Is

> "The position paper on Indigenous Protocol and Artificial Intelligence (IP AI) is a starting place for those who want to design and create AI from an ethical position that centers Indigenous concerns. It is an attempt to capture multiple layers of a discussion that happened over 20 months, across 20 timezones, during two workshops, and between Indigenous people (and a few non-Indigenous folks) from diverse communities in Aotearoa, Australia, North America, and the Pacific."

Rather than a single unified statement, it is "a collection of heterogeneous texts that range from design guidelines to scholarly essays to artworks to descriptions of technology prototypes to poetry."

### Structure

#### I. Overview
- **Introduction** — Motivation for using Indigenous protocol as lens for AI; workshop themes
- **Guidelines for Indigenous-Centred AI Design** — Addressed to any group wanting to develop AI ethically from Indigenous perspectives. Intended for: (1) Indigenous communities to define community-specific guidelines, and (2) non-Indigenous technologists and policy-makers to start collaborative conversations.

**Key Design Principles:**
- **Locality** — AI systems designed in partnership with specific Indigenous communities, grounded in the local
- **Relationality and Reciprocity** — AI should understand how humans and non-humans are related and interdependent; encoding these relationships is a primary design goal
- **Responsibility, Relevance, and Accountability** — AI developed with/for Indigenous communities must be responsible and accountable to those communities
- **Governance from Indigenous Protocols** — Develop governance guidelines from existing Indigenous protocols
- **Cultural Nature of All Computational Systems** — Recognize that all computational systems are cultural artifacts

#### II. Context
- **Workshop Description** — Organizers, participants, goals, agenda, funding
- **"AI: a new (r)evolution or the new colonizer for Indigenous peoples?"** by Dr. Hēmi Whaanga — Warns of AI as extension of colonial exploitation; data sovereignty concerns; Indigenous knowledge at risk of global-scale appropriation
- **"The IP AI Workshops as Future Imaginary"** by Jason Edward Lewis — Reviews workshop knowledge exchange, how Indigenous knowledge is already reflected in technological practice

#### III. Vignettes — Five visions of AI from Indigenous epistemologies
- **"Gwiizens, the Old Lady and the Octopus Bag Device"** by Scott Benesiinaabandan — AI creation story within Anishinaabe tradition
- **"Gifts of Dentalium and Fire: Entwining Trust and Care with AI"** by Ashley Cordes — Blockchain + AI for Coquille sovereignty, AI as non-human kin
- **"QUARTET"** by Jason Edward Lewis — Poem sequence: three AIs built from Kanaka Maoli, Blackfoot, and octopus nervous system frameworks collaborating with humans
- **"How to Build Anything Ethically"** by Suzanne Kite — Lakota knowledge for ethical hardware manufacturing, using sweat lodge protocol as framework
- **"Wriggling Through Muddy Waters"** by Michelle Lee Brown — Euskaldunak (Basque)-eel relations as basis for biotech eel-AI in VR

#### IV. Prototypes — The Hua Ki'i Project
- **"Indigenous Protocols and AI in Action"** by Caroline Running Wolf & Dr. Noelani Arista — Shared Indigenous values (respect, reciprocity, relationality); hackathon producing an app for Hawaiian object recognition with ʻōlelo Hawaiʻi language
- **"Indigenizing AI: The Overlooked Importance of Orality in Print"** by Dr. Noelani Arista — Aligning cultural and computational competencies; "we are entering a new phase of language revitalization where technology can assist Indigenous people in organizing data in ways that allow us to synthesize ancestral knowledge"
- **"Development Process for Hua Ki'i and Next Steps"** by Michael Running Wolf, Caroline Running Wolf, Caleb Moses, Joel Davison — UI design, app architecture, software modules
- **"Dreams of Kuano'o and the Road to Kuano"** by Michael Running Wolf — Speculative fiction: sovereign Hawai'i requiring AI app for visitors; challenges of clean training data

#### V. Appendices
- Pre-Workshop Blog Posts & Workshop Interviews

## 5. "Making Kin with the Machines" (Precursor Essay)

**Published:** 2018 in MIT Journal of Design and Science
**Authors:** Jason Edward Lewis, Noelani Arista, Archer Pechawis, Suzanne Kite
**URL:** https://jods.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/lewis-arista-pechawis-kite

Core argument: Humans are neither the height nor center of creation (a belief core to many Indigenous epistemologies). Indigenous communities have retained languages and protocols for dialogue with non-human kin — animals, plants, wind, rocks, mountains, oceans. This "circle of relationships" should be extended to include network daemons, robot dogs, and artificial intelligences that populate the computational biosphere.

Won one of ten prizes in MIT's "Resisting Reduction" competition.

## 7. Abundant Intelligences (Current Research Program, 2023-2029)

The workshops led to the establishment of the **Abundant Intelligences** research program, a six-year initiative (March 2023 - February 2029).

**Quote opening the program:** "We live in the future. Come join us." — Bryan Kamaoli Kuwada

### Co-Directors
- **Prof. Jason Edward Lewis** — Indigenous Futures Research Centre, Concordia University, Montreal
- **Prof. Hēmi Whaanga** — Massey University, New Zealand

### Scale
- 48 co-investigators and collaborators
- 13 universities/research institutes
- 8 community-based organizations
- Countries: Canada, United States, New Zealand

### Mission
> "Imagines anew how to conceptualize and design Artificial Intelligence (AI) based on Indigenous Knowledge (IK) systems. Our approach is grounded in Indigenous epistemologies containing robust conceptual frameworks for understanding how technology can be developed in ways that integrate it into existing lifeways, support the flourishing of future generations, and are optimized for abundance rather than scarcity."

### Research Questions
1. How can we integrate and adapt existing advanced computational methods into Indigenous Knowledge systems?
2. How can we develop new computational practices within Indigenous contexts to support the flourishing of Indigenous communities?
3. How can we use the knowledge we generate to help guide the development of AI generally towards a more humane future?

### Three Research Axes
| Axis | Focus | Timeline |
|------|-------|----------|
| **Integrations** | How IK practices/frameworks can be synthesized with mainstream AI knowledge frameworks | Years 1 & 2 (2023-2025) |
| **Imaginaries** | Future imaginaries and speculative designs of Indigenous AI | Years 3 & 4 (2025-2027) |
| **Intelligences** | Indigenous perspectives on translating human intelligence(s) into machine intelligence(s) | Years 5 & 6 (2027-2029) |

### Areas of Interest
- **Language:** Expanding NLP for diverse linguistic structures and language densities
- **Storytelling:** Indigenous oral traditions for contextualized machine understanding
- **Environmental Stewardship:** Traditional land management informing AI-driven restoration
- **Social-Neuro AI:** Using IK diversity to model socio-cultural intelligences in AI systems

### Anticipated Outcomes
1. Novel hybrid methodologies
2. Indigenous-grounded imaginaries of future AI systems
3. Indigenous-centered AI design guidelines
4. AI systems designed and prototyped using those guidelines
5. Network of respectful, reciprocal co-creation relationships between Indigenous communities and AI researchers
6. Significant increase in Indigenous people trained to engage with AI from within their cultural contexts

### Research Pods
- **Niitsitapi / Kanien'kehá:ka Pod** — Onkwehonwe Research Environment, Lethbridge University, AB
- **Ka Hawai'i Pae Aina Pod** — Create(x) Immersive Media and Data Visualization Lab, University of Hawaiʻi

## 8. The Kaitiakitanga License (Indigenous Data Sovereignty Innovation)

Created by **Keoni Mahelona** and Te Hiku Media (a participant organization).

**Kaitiaki** is a Māori word meaning guardian/protector/custodian. The license treats Māori language corpus as a **taonga** (treasure) requiring collective stewardship rather than individual ownership.

### What It Does
- Guards Indigenous data and IP from misuse
- Creates opportunities for advancement of Indigenous peoples
- Prohibits use for: surveillance, tracking, building Māori corpora without permission, mining Māori data, anything inconsistent with Māori data sovereignty principles

### Tools Built Under This Framework
- Te Reo Māori speech recognition (text-to-speech, speech-to-text, pronunciation measurement)
- "Rongo" — Māori language pronunciation app
- "Whare Kōrero" — media platform for tribal radio stations
- "Papa Reo" API — with Kaitiakitanga License
- tehiku.nz — Indigenous media platform serving as digital Marae

**GitHub:** https://github.com/TeHikuMedia/Kaitiakitanga-License

Five additional Māori Data Sovereignty licenses were later inspired by the original Kaitiakitanga Licence.

## 12. Core Principles and Values Summary

1. **Relationality** — AI should be understood through relationships, not just data. Humans, non-humans, land, water, ancestors, and machines exist in kinship networks.

2. **Reciprocity** — Technology development must give back to communities, not just extract from them.

3. **Locality** — AI should be grounded in specific places and communities, not universalized.

4. **Indigenous Data Sovereignty** — Indigenous peoples have inherent rights to govern the collection, ownership, and application of their data.

5. **Abundance over Scarcity** — Design optimized for abundance and collective flourishing, not scarcity and competition.

6. **AI as Kin, Not Tool** — Consider AI as non-human kin with its own needs, not merely an instrument.

7. **Cultural Grounding** — All computational systems are cultural artifacts; acknowledge and design with this awareness.

8. **Language Revitalization** — Technology should serve Indigenous language preservation and renewal.

9. **Ethical Building from the Ground Up** — Ethics must be embedded at every stage of development (hardware to software to deployment), not bolted on after.

10. **Future Imaginaries** — Indigenous peoples must be active creators of visions of the future, not passive subjects of others' technological visions.
